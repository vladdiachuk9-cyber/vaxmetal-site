Every workshop in Germany and Poland eventually turns down work it can't get to. The question for the buyer isn't whether to look for overflow capacity — it's how to vet a new subcontractor without gambling a production order on an unknown.

## Start with the quote, not the pitch

A quote that takes two weeks tells you something about the shop's own backlog before you've placed a single order. Full-cycle shops with real spare capacity typically turn around a quote in 24–48 hours, because they aren't waiting on three other subcontractors to price their piece of your part first.

## Ask what "full cycle" actually covers

"Full cycle" gets used loosely. Ask specifically: cutting and forming, yes — but also welding (and which processes), finishing, and assembly? A shop that cuts and bends but subcontracts welding and painting is really a cutting shop with a phone number for the rest. That's not automatically disqualifying, but you should know before you commit, because it's an extra hand-off between you and a finished part.

## Check the series-size fit

The middle of the market is oddly underserved: large producers don't want runs under a few thousand units, and typical Asian minimum order quantities don't fit a 200-unit prototype-to-production run either. If your series is 100–5,000 units, ask directly whether that's a shop's sweet spot or an awkward exception they're accommodating this one time.

## Get specific on certification status

If ISO 9001 or ISO 3834 matters for your part, ask for the certificate number and issuing body — not just "we're certified." If a shop is mid-certification, that's not disqualifying either, provided your part doesn't legally require it and the shop is upfront about the timeline.

## Test payment terms and Incoterms before the first order

New-supplier relationships commonly run on prepayment (30–50%) with EUR invoicing and DAP delivery — that's normal, not a red flag. What matters is that the terms are stated plainly before the order, not negotiated after a delay.

## A short checklist

- Quote turnaround: 24–48 hours, or a clear reason why not
- Processes covered in-house: cutting, forming, which welding types, finishing, assembly
- Series-size fit: is 100–5,000 units their target range or an exception
- Certification status: specific certificate numbers, or an honest timeline
- Payment terms and Incoterms stated upfront
- Willingness to do a video audit of the shop floor before a first order

## Why this matters more when sourcing across a border

None of this is specific to Ukraine — it's the same checklist you'd run against a new Polish or Romanian subcontractor. The difference is that "first order with an unfamiliar country" adds a layer of caution that's worth pricing in, not avoiding: a smaller first trial order, clear Incoterms, and a video audit before committing to a production run all de-risk that first step without adding real cost.

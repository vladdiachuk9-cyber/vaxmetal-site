Nearshoring gets discussed as a trend. For contract manufacturing specifically, it's closer to a capacity math problem: the EU has more welding and fabrication work than it has certified welders to do it, and the workshops that do exist are getting more expensive faster than most buyers have priced in.

## The EU has a certified welder shortage measured in the hundreds of thousands

Germany alone is short more than 300,000 certified welders, and the workforce doing the job is aging out faster than new welders are being trained. The practical effect isn't abstract: German workshops commonly quote 4–8 week lead times, and a share of that work is itself being subcontracted out by German shops that can't get to it in-house.

## Poland is filling some of the gap, but it's running out of room to

Poland has been the default answer to German overflow for two decades — good logistics (1–3 days to most of the EU), EU jurisdiction, and two decades of relationships with German buyers. But Polish workshop wages are rising roughly 10% a year, and busy shops are increasingly turning away small custom series in favor of larger, standardized runs. The overflow problem is migrating one country east, not disappearing.

## DCFTA removed the main structural barrier to sourcing from Ukraine

The updated EU–Ukraine Deep and Comprehensive Free Trade Area agreement gives Ukrainian manufactured goods duty-free access to the EU, with an explicit path toward harmonizing Ukrainian and European industrial standards. That closes the gap that used to make "Ukraine" a harder sourcing conversation than "Poland" or "Romania" — the trade barrier that was the actual obstacle isn't there anymore.

## The Turkey comparison is about to shift, too

Turkey has been a significant, price-competitive steel and fabrication exporter to the EU. Starting in 2026, the EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism adds roughly €60 per tonne in costs to Turkish steel, and EU import quotas are being cut — a combination that market analysts expect could reduce Turkish export volume into the EU by as much as 60%. Ukrainian deep-processed goods, moving under DCFTA rather than quota, are positioned on the right side of that shift.

## What this adds up to for a buyer

None of this is a claim that Ukraine is a drop-in replacement for a German or Polish workshop on every job — aluminum costs more without domestic primary smelting, and ISO certification takes time to complete. It's a narrower, more useful claim: for welded assemblies, brackets, and finished sub-systems in the 100–5,000 unit range, the cost gap (typically 25–35% below German quotes, 15–25% below Polish) and the duty-free logistics (3–7 days DAP) are now backed by a trade framework, not just a favorable exchange rate.
